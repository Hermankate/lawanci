Legacy for African Women and Children Initiative (LAWANCI) is a national Non-Governmental Organization incorporated on the 13th February 2018 in the Republic of South Sudan with the main aim of empowering, encouraging and promoting the safety of women and children through education, economic, health and psychological support services across the continent.
The mission for Legacy for African Women and Children Initiative (LAWANCI) is to empower, encourage and promote the safety of children and women through education, economic, health and psychological support services across the continent.
The vision Legacy for African Women and Children Initiative (LAWANCI) is to create an African society that recognizes the legacy of women and children.

Our Objectives

What We Aim to Achieve

Empowerment

To empower women and children through education, skill development, and economic opportunities.

Health & Safety

To promote health and ensure the safety of women and children by providing necessary health services and awareness programs.

Advocacy

To advocate for the rights of women and children and work towards eliminating discrimination and violence.

Issues of Interest

Key Areas We Focus On

Gender Equality

Promoting gender equality and empowering women in all spheres of life through advocacy and education.

Child Protection

Ensuring the safety and welfare of children by fighting against child abuse, trafficking, and exploitation.

Access to Education

Advocating for quality education for all, with a focus on marginalized communities and vulnerable groups.

Health Awareness

Improving health outcomes by raising awareness on critical health issues and providing access to healthcare services.

Economic Empowerment

Supporting economic independence for women through vocational training and entrepreneurship programs.

Environmental Sustainability

Promoting environmental awareness and sustainability practices to protect our planet for future generations.

History of South Sudan

Understanding Our Roots

Early History

South Sudan has a rich cultural heritage dating back thousands of years, with diverse tribes and a strong tradition of oral storytelling that preserves its early history.

Colonial Era

The region underwent significant changes during the colonial period, which saw the imposition of external control and the drawing of new national boundaries.

Struggle for Independence

Decades of conflict marked the struggle for independence from Sudan, culminating in a referendum that led to the establishment of South Sudan as an independent nation in 2011.

Post-Independence Challenges

Since gaining independence, South Sudan has faced numerous challenges, including political instability, economic hardship, and ongoing ethnic conflicts, but it remains committed to building a peaceful and prosperous future.

Impact of the civil wars

The civil wars in South Sudan have been a series of complex conflicts rooted in ethnic tensions, political power struggles, and issues related to resources and governance.
South Sudan has experienced two major civil wars since its independence from Sudan in 2011.

First Sudanese Civil War (1955-1972)

Before the independence of South Sudan, the region was involved in conflicts with the central Sudanese government. The First Sudanese Civil War (1955-1972) began before Sudan’s independence from British-Egyptian rule in 1956. This war was primarily fought between the northern Sudanese government, dominated by Arab Muslims, and the southern Sudanese, who were predominantly African Christians and animists. The conflict was driven by political, religious, and ethnic differences. The war ended with the signing of the **Addis Ababa Agreement** in 1972, granting the south a degree of autonomy.

Second Sudanese Civil War (1983-2005)

The Second Sudanese Civil War (1983-2005) resumed hostilities, driven by the Sudanese government's decision to impose Sharia law across the country and the abrogation of the Addis Ababa Agreement. The Sudan People’s Liberation Movement/Army (SPLM/A), led by John Garang, fought for greater autonomy for the south and later for full independence. The conflict was marked by extreme violence, famine, and human rights abuses, resulting in the deaths of an estimated 2 million people and the displacement of millions more.
This war ended with the signing of the Comprehensive Peace Agreement (CPA) in 2005, which paved the way for the eventual independence of South Sudan. The CPA granted South Sudan autonomy for six years, followed by a referendum on independence. In 2011, the people of South Sudan voted overwhelmingly in favor of independence, leading to the creation of the Republic of South Sudan on July 9, 2011.

South Sudanese Civil War (2013-2018)

Just two years after gaining independence, South Sudan descended into civil war in December 2013. The conflict erupted due to a power struggle between President Salva Kiir, an ethnic Dinka, and his former vice president, Riek Machar, an ethnic Nuer. The political rivalry quickly escalated into an armed conflict with ethnic overtones, leading to widespread violence between the Dinka and Nuer communities.
The civil war was characterized by mass atrocities, including killings, sexual violence, and the use of child soldiers. The conflict created a humanitarian crisis, with millions of people displaced internally and as refugees in neighboring countries. Several attempts at peace negotiations were made, but they often broke down, leading to renewed fighting.

Peace Agreements and Continued Instability

In August 2015, a peace agreement known as the Agreement on the Resolution of the Conflict in the Republic of South Sudan (ARCSS) was signed. However, the agreement collapsed in July 2016 when fighting erupted in the capital, Juba, leading to further violence and displacement.

A more significant breakthrough came with the signing of the Revitalized Agreement on the Resolution of the Conflict in South Sudan (R-ARCSS) in September 2018. This agreement aimed to create a power-sharing government, with Salva Kiir remaining as President and Riek Machar reinstated as Vice President.

Current Situation

Since the signing of the R-ARCSS, there has been a fragile peace in South Sudan. The transitional government was formed in February 2020, but the situation remains unstable with sporadic violence and ongoing humanitarian challenges. Issues such as disarmament, security sector reform, and the return of refugees and internally displaced persons are critical challenges that need to be addressed to ensure lasting peace and stability in South Sudan.

The country continues to face significant obstacles, including economic challenges, ethnic rivalries, and a lack of infrastructure, making peace and development efforts arduous. The international community, including the African Union and the United Nations, continues to support peace efforts and humanitarian assistance to address the ongoing crises in South Sudan.
