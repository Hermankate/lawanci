Legacy for African Women and Children Initiative (LAWANCI) is a national Non-Governmental Organization incorporated on the 13th February 2018 in the Republic of South Sudan with the main aim of empowering, encouraging and promoting the safety of women and children through education, economic, health and psychological support services across the continent.
The mission for Legacy for African Women and Children Initiative (LAWANCI) is to empower, encourage and promote the safety of children and women through education, economic, health and psychological support services across the continent.
The vision Legacy for African Women and Children Initiative (LAWANCI) is to create an African society that recognizes the legacy of women and children.

Our Objectives

What We Aim to Achieve

Empowerment

To empower women and children through education, skill development, and economic opportunities.

Health & Safety

To promote health and ensure the safety of women and children by providing necessary health services and awareness programs.

Advocacy

To advocate for the rights of women and children and work towards eliminating discrimination and violence.

Issues of Interest

Key Areas We Focus On

Gender Equality

Promoting gender equality and empowering women in all spheres of life through advocacy and education.

Child Protection

Ensuring the safety and welfare of children by fighting against child abuse, trafficking, and exploitation.

Access to Education

Advocating for quality education for all, with a focus on marginalized communities and vulnerable groups.

Health Awareness

Improving health outcomes by raising awareness on critical health issues and providing access to healthcare services.

Economic Empowerment

Supporting economic independence for women through vocational training and entrepreneurship programs.

Environmental Sustainability

Promoting environmental awareness and sustainability practices to protect our planet for future generations.

History of South Sudan

Understanding Our Roots

Early History

South Sudan has a rich cultural heritage dating back thousands of years, with diverse tribes and a strong tradition of oral storytelling that preserves its early history.

Colonial Era

The region underwent significant changes during the colonial period, which saw the imposition of external control and the drawing of new national boundaries.

Struggle for Independence

Decades of conflict marked the struggle for independence from Sudan, culminating in a referendum that led to the establishment of South Sudan as an independent nation in 2011.

Post-Independence Challenges

Since gaining independence, South Sudan has faced numerous challenges, including political instability, economic hardship, and ongoing ethnic conflicts, but it remains committed to building a peaceful and prosperous future.

Impact of the Civil wars

In South Sudan, conflict and war have deep and long-standing roots ,that have been influenced by historical, ethnic, political, and economic factors. Here’s a detailed overview of the conflict in 2013 and 2016, focusing on its background, causes, developments, and consequences.

Historical Context

1. Colonial Legacy: The roots of the conflict can be traced back to Sudan's colonial history. British colonial rule (1899-1956) created division between the Northern and Southern regions of Sudan. The North, predominantly Muslim and Arab, were given greater administrative and economic advantages, while the South, largely Christian and indigenous, was deliberately marginalized.

2. First and Second Civil Wars: The post-independence period was marked by internal strife. The First Sudanese Civil War (1955-1972) stemmed from tensions between the North and South, leading to significant casualties. The second phase, the Second Sudanese Civil War (1983-2005) resulted in millions of deaths. The war ended with the signing of the Comprehensive Peace Agreement (CPA) between the Government of Sudan and the Sudan People’s Liberation Movement (SPLM) in 2005, granting autonomy to the South and establishing a timeline for a referendum.

3. Independence: South Sudan gained independence from Sudan in July 2011, becoming the world's newest nation. However, independence did not bring peace; instead, it highlighted deep-seated ethnic and political divides.

Ethnic and Political Context

South Sudan is home to hundreds of ethnic groups, including Dinka and Nuer, which have historically vied for power. Tensions between these groups have been exacerbated by leadership struggles, particularly within the Sudan People's Liberation Movement (SPLM), the dominant political party.

The 2013 Conflict

1. Political Tensions: The immediate cause of the 2013 conflict can be traced back to political rivalry between President Salva Kiir (a Dinka) and his former deputy, Riek Machar (a Nuer). After a power struggle, Kiir dismissed Machar in July 2013, polarizing the political landscape along ethnic lines.

2. Outbreak of Violence: On December 15, 2013, violence erupted in Juba, the capital, following an attempted coup against Kiir. This violence quickly spiraled into ethnic violence, with Dinka soldiers attacking Nuer civilians. The situation deteriorated rapidly, leading to widespread killings, displacement, and human rights abuses.

3. Escalation of Conflict: The fighting spread beyond Juba to other regions, with armed groups representing different ethnicities forming and aligning with either Kiir or Machar. The humanitarian situation worsened, with millions displaced, famine conditions arising, and significant atrocities reported.

4. Interventions and Peace Efforts: The African Union and regional bodies, such as the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD), attempted to mediate. A ceasefire was signed in January 2014, but it was repeatedly violated, and fighting continued.

The 2016 Conflict

1. Renewed Violence:Tensions remained high after an uneasy peace agreement was signed in August 2015. Fighting broke out again in July 2016, particularly in Juba, as forces loyal to Machar clashed with government troops. This incident resulted in another heavy civilian toll and raised fears of a renewed full-scale war.

2. Political Dynamics: the July clashes, Machar was forced to flee the country, leading to a significant military reshuffle. The government claimed victory, but the underlying tensions remained unresolved. Several factions emerged, complicating any potential route to peace.

3. Humanitarian Crisis: The renewed conflict exacerbated the humanitarian crisis in South Sudan, with millions suffering from hunger, lack of medical care, and displacement. Reports of ethnic cleansing and mass atrocities were widespread, attracting international condemnation.

4. International Response: The international community, including the United Nations, continued to urge for peace negotiations. Sanctions were imposed on various leaders involved in the conflict, but these efforts met with limited success.

Consequences

- Humanitarian Disaster: The conflict has led to one of the world’s worst humanitarian crises, with millions of people internally displaced or seeking refuge in neighboring countries. Hunger and disease are widespread across South Sudan.
- Economic Collapse: The war severely disrupted the already fragile economy, heavily reliant on oil revenues. The country has faced rampant inflation and a lack of basic services.
- Fragmentation of Society: Ethnic divisions have deepened, leading to further mistrust and animosity among groups; some of which had just started to rebuild themselves after independence.
- International Isolation: South Sudan became increasingly isolated, facing condemnation for human rights abuses and ineffective governance.

Conclusion

The conflicts in 2013 and 2016 in South Sudan illustrate the fragility of the world's youngest nation. A complex mix of unresolved historical grievances, deep-seated and long-standing ethnic tensions, and power struggles continues to challenge peace efforts and stability. The international community's role remains crucial in mediating peace and addressing humanitarian needs, but a sustainable solution will require addressing the underlying causes of conflict, and fostering inclusive governance.

Role of Women In SouthSudan

Women in South Sudan play a crucial and multifaceted role in both the ongoing conflict and society at large. Despite being disproportionately affected by violence, including sexual violence used as a weapon of war, South Sudanese women have emerged as vital agents of change and resilience within their communities. They often serve as primary caregivers and breadwinners, navigating immense challenges such as food insecurity and displacement while striving to support their families. Organizations like Women for Women International highlight how these women are not merely victims; they actively engage in peacebuilding efforts and advocate for their rights, seeking to transform their circumstances through economic empowerment and social change initiatives. Their leadership is essential for fostering a more equitable and democratic future in South Sudan, as they work tirelessly to address the gender-based violence that has become endemic in the context of prolonged conflict.
